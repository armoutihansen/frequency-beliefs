# Domain Review

**Document:** paper/main.tex (with sections under paper/sections/)
**Date:** 2026-05-25
**Lenses applied:** 5/5

## Summary

The mathematical content of the paper is, on the whole, sound. The three headline characterisations — squared-distance (Proposition 1), frequency-guessing (Proposition 2), and Manhattan (Proposition 3) — are correctly proved, with sharp endpoint constructions that I verified algebraically for every case, and the Lemma 1 transport argument is rigorous. The closed-form/semi-analytical/grid-only taxonomy that the project enforces (CLAUDE.md) is preserved cleanly in §3, the design comparison §4, and the discussion of Hamming/Chebyshev. The code in `scripts/verify_regions.py` and `scripts/design_efficiency.py` implements the same objects the paper characterises and runs LP/threshold/root-find checks that cross-validate the closed-form formulas. The main issues I found are minor wording slips that risk eroding the analytical-vs-numerical distinction, two small but worth-flagging proof tightenings, and one place where the abstract slightly over-summarises Manhattan's bound status. Nothing in the audit calls a theorem statement into question; all flagged items are exposition or boundary clarifications.

### Positive Findings

- The sufficiency direction of the squared-distance characterisation (Appendix A.2) uses a clean integrality bound ‖d‖² ≥ 2M that I verified line by line — this is exactly the right argument and is presented economically.
- All four "sharp endpoint" constructions in Appendix A.2 (lower with r_i > 0, upper with r_i > 0, upper with r_i = 0; lower with r_i = 0 is trivial) were checked by me against every constraint of P_Q(r) and the simplex; all constructions are correct and the proposed extremals indeed lie in P_Q(r).
- The marginal-cost identity Δℓ_i(t; p_i) = 2 F_i(t; n, p_i) − 1 for Manhattan (08_appendix.tex:259-261) is correct; it is also tested directly in `verify_regions.py` (the r6 check).
- The Hamming counterexample at 07_discussion.tex:88-90 ((3,0,0,0,0) vs (0,0,1,1,1) at n=3, k=5, uniform p): the expected Hamming scores are 257/125 and 272/125 respectively, with single-count transfer (2,1,0,0,0) giving 252/125 < 257/125. I verified this independently. The example correctly demonstrates that single-count transfer optimality is necessary but not sufficient for Hamming.

## Notation Consistency

Notation is consistent across sections. Key conventions:
- `p ∈ Δ^k` for latent beliefs, `r ∈ Ω_N` for count report, `ω ∈ Ω_N` for realised counts — used uniformly.
- `R_S(p)` for optimal-report correspondence, `P_S(r)` for identified set — uniform.
- Subscript convention: `P_Q`, `P_0`, `P_M`, `P_H` for the four rule-specific identified sets — uniform.
- `F_i(t; n, p_i)` for binomial CDF — consistent boundary conventions (F_i(-1) = 0, F_i(n) = 1) stated at first use in §3.4.
- `m = m(r) = |{j : r_j > 0}|` defined at first use (03_scoring_rules.tex:82) and used consistently in Corollary 1 and the appendix.

One minor item: the abstract uses "Frequency-guessing scoring" while §3.3 also calls it "the frequency-guessing mechanism of Schlag–Tremewan" and "fixed-prize" interchangeably. This is fine but ensure consistency on the next pass — the discussion section uses both "frequency-guessing" and the implied "exact-match" reading depending on context.
