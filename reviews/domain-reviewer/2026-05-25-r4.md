## Item 1 — Lemma `lem:l1-linear` statement (03_scoring_rules.tex:304-315)

### Tightness audit

**Main equality** sup_{p∈P_M(r)} Σ a_i p_i = sup_{c∈[0,1]} sup_{p∈Σ(c)} Σ a_i p_i:
- Foundation: Proposition 3 gives p∈P_M(r) ⟺ ∃ c∈[0,1] with F_i(r_i-1;n,p_i) ≤ c ≤ F_i(r_i;n,p_i) ∀i. Inverting the binomial CDFs (using the monotonicity established at 08_appendix.tex:284-297) gives p_v^{(L)}(c) ≤ p_v ≤ p_v^{(U)}(c). So P_M(r) = ⋃_c Σ(c) holds exactly. ✓
- Sup over union = sup of suprema: this is a universal fact for any function, no monotonicity required. ✓
- The inner problem sup over Σ(c) is a linear program in a box-simplex slice (closed and bounded, hence the LP attains its optimum when Σ(c) is non-empty). ✓

**Quantile-index identities** at lines 312-314:
- min_p j*_τ(p) = min{j : sup_p S_j(p) ≥ τ}: j*_τ(p) = min{j : S_j(p)≥τ}, so j*_τ(p) ≤ j ⟺ S_j(p) ≥ τ. Hence min_p j*_τ(p) ≤ j ⟺ ∃ p ∈ P_M(r) with S_j(p) ≥ τ ⟺ sup_p S_j(p) ≥ τ. ✓
- max_p j*_τ(p) = max{j : inf_p S_{j-1}(p) < τ}: j*_τ(p) ≥ j ⟺ S_{j-1}(p) < τ (strict), so max_p j*_τ(p) ≥ j ⟺ ∃ p with S_{j-1}(p) < τ ⟺ inf_p S_{j-1}(p) < τ. The strict-vs.-weak inequality is the right convention for j* = min{j : S_j ≥ τ} and is handled correctly. ✓
- One subtle point: the displayed identities use strict `<` on `inf_p S_{j-1}(p) < τ`. Since inf over the compact P_M(r) is attained (P_M is closed and bounded, S_{j-1} is continuous), `inf < τ` ⟺ ∃ p with `S_{j-1}(p) < τ`, so this is rigorous.

### Positive findings

- The lemma's reach is correctly restricted to *linear* functionals Σ a_i p_i. The paper does not over-generalize to arbitrary functionals here (that broader claim lives in the §3.6 framework paragraph at lines 296-300, where it is appropriately hedged by "the same operations apply on each slice").

This is the cleanest derivation of multi-category median/quantile bounds the paper has presented.

## Item 4 — §3.6 framework statement (03_scoring_rules.tex:285-300, 340)

### Audit of the variance claim

The framework paragraph at lines 296-300 states:
> "Squared-distance and frequency-guessing scoring induce polytope identified sets, so linear T (mean) reduces to a linear program, quadratic T (variance) to a quadratic program, and a step-function T (median or quantile) to a sequence of feasibility linear programs."

And the table footnote at line 336:
> "Variance bounds follow from a quadratic program over the same identified sets and are reported in the supplementary verification output."

The subtlety: Var(Y) = Σ x_i^2 p_i − (Σ x_i p_i)^2 is a **concave** quadratic function of p (the quadratic part −(a·p)^2 is concave, the linear part is linear). On a polytope:
- **min Var(Y)** is a convex quadratic minimization — a true QP, polynomial-time solvable. ✓
- **max Var(Y)** is *concave maximization* — equivalent to a (non-convex) QP, generally NP-hard, though the maximum is always attained at a vertex of the polytope and so reduces to vertex enumeration over P_S(r). This is qualitatively harder than "a quadratic program."

For the small examples in Table `tab:functional-menu` (n=10, k=5), vertex enumeration is computationally trivial; for larger n and k, vertex enumeration of the polytope (or even just the bound-relevant vertices) is feasible but is *not* what is meant by "a quadratic program" in the standard convex-optimization sense. The Manhattan case adds an outer threshold parameter c, so it becomes a one-parameter family of concave-maximization problems over box-simplex slices — also handled by vertex enumeration on each slice plus a grid sweep.

### Issues

| # | Issue | Severity | Location |
|---|-------|----------|----------|
| V1 | The variance bound is described as "a quadratic program," but max Var(Y) on a polytope is concave maximization (non-convex QP); only min Var(Y) is a standard convex QP. For polytope rules this still reduces to vertex enumeration (tractable for small n, k), and for the Manhattan rule it reduces to vertex enumeration per threshold-slice plus a c-grid sweep. The paper's framing as "a quadratic program" is acceptable shorthand for an applied audience but understates the algorithmic distinction between the variance lower and upper bounds. **What would change my mind:** a half-sentence noting that the variance upper bound is concave maximization, attained at a vertex (or, in the Manhattan case, at a vertex of some Σ(c) slice), while the lower bound is a convex QP — keeping the "same identified set" framing but being honest about the dual algorithmic regimes. | MINOR | 03_scoring_rules.tex:298, 336 |

### Positive findings

- The three-regime taxonomy (no x / ordinal x / numerical x) at lines 290-294 is the right framing — it clarifies that variance and mean bounds require numerical x while quantile bounds need only an ordering. This matches the standard partial-identification literature on quantile bounds.
- The framework paragraph correctly distinguishes the polytope rules from Manhattan ("Manhattan-distance scoring induces a threshold-indexed slice family rather than a polytope, but the same operations apply on each slice and the optimum is then taken over the threshold parameter"). This is the right level of generality.

The abstract sentence at line 41 reads:
> "For each rule the same identified set yields bounds on variance, median, and quantile functionals of the induced outcome distribution via the same linear, quadratic, or feasibility programs over a single underlying region."

This is correct at the qualitative level claimed. The "single underlying region" framing is the right one-line summary of the §3.6 unification.
