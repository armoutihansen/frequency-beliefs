## Item 2 — Lemma `lem:manhattan-unimodal` proof revision (08_appendix.tex:320)

### Audit of the corrected description

The prior r2 description had a wrong directional claim ("both arguments weakly decreasing"). The new wording reads:

> The first argument \(p_i^{(U)}(c)\) is continuous and weakly decreasing in \(c\), while the second argument \(1-\sum_{v\ne i}p_v^{(L)}(c)\) is continuous and weakly increasing in \(c\) (since each \(p_v^{(L)}\) is weakly decreasing); their unique crossing is the \(c^*\) where \(p_i^{(U)}(c^*)+\sum_{v\ne i}p_v^{(L)}(c^*)=1\), and \(\phi\) is unimodal in \(c\) with peak at \(c^*\).

Verification:
- \(p_i^{(U)}(c)\): largest \(p\) with \(F(r_i;n,p)\ge c\). As \(c\) rises, the constraint tightens, so \(p_i^{(U)}(c)\) is weakly decreasing. ✓
- \(p_v^{(L)}(c)\): smallest \(p\) with \(F(r_v-1;n,p)\le c\). As \(c\) rises, more \(p\) satisfy the constraint and the infimum weakly decreases, hence \(\sum p_v^{(L)}(c)\) weakly decreases and \(1-\sum p_v^{(L)}(c)\) weakly increases. ✓
- The "min of (decreasing, increasing)" structure is the textbook unimodal envelope, with peak at the crossing point. ✓
- Uniqueness of \(c^*\): LHS \(=p_i^{(U)}(c)+\sum_{v\ne i}p_v^{(L)}(c)\) is continuous and strictly decreasing (as r2 verified via the \(r_i<n\) hypothesis), so the IVT root is unique. ✓

## Item 3 — New appendix subsection: Robustness to asymmetric beliefs (08_appendix.tex:376-401)

### Audit of the conclusion

The subsection reports win shares for three asymmetric Dirichlet concentration vectors at \(n=20, k=5\):

| α | Squared (avg-coord / lin-mean) | Min component |
|---|---|---|
| (5, 0.5, 0.5, 0.5, 0.5) | 0.60 / 0.72 | 0.5 (×4) |
| (3, 3, 0.3, 0.3, 0.3)   | 0.76 / 0.82 | 0.3 (×3) |
| (5, 2, 1, 0.5, 0.2)     | 0.67 / 0.86 | 0.2 |

Squared-distance dominates in every cell on both targets. The paper interprets this as: "the governing variable is the marginal concentration of the draws, not the symmetry of the Dirichlet itself."

Marginal-concentration check: for Dirichlet(α), the marginal of \(p_i\) is Beta(\(\alpha_i, \alpha_0-\alpha_i\)). For \(\alpha_i\le 0.5\) with \(\alpha_0\) of order 7, the marginal is U-shaped or peaked at 0, i.e., marginally concentrated near 0. All three test vectors have at least one component \(\le 0.5\) — explicitly noted in the caption ("each of the three vectors has at least one component \(\alpha_i\le 0.5\), so the resulting beliefs are marginally concentrated"). The link from "marginal concentration of a small-α component" to "squared-distance wins" is then the same one §4 established for symmetric small-α.

### Issues

| # | Issue | Severity | Location |
|---|-------|----------|----------|
| AB1 | The three asymmetric vectors confound asymmetry with marginal concentration: every tested vector has at least one small (\(\le 0.5\)) component. A control case — an asymmetric vector with *no* small component, e.g., \(\alpha=(5,4,3,2,2)\) (all \(\alpha_i\ge 2\), \(\alpha_0=16\)) — would isolate asymmetry from concentration and let the "concentration, not symmetry" attribution stand on a contrasting cell rather than only on a constructive one. The current evidence is *consistent with* the marginal-concentration story (the only mechanism shared across the three tested vectors that aligns with §4's regime) but does not *exclusively* support it: the alternative reading "squared-distance just wins under any non-uniform asymmetric vector" is not ruled out by the table. The paper's interpretation is the right one given the §4 mechanism, but the appendix test does not on its own pin it down. **What would change my mind:** adding a fourth row with all \(\alpha_i\ge 2\) (no marginal concentration) showing frequency-guessing dominate or the ranking change. | MINOR | 08_appendix.tex:381-395 |

### Positive findings

- The caption itself explicitly notes the small-component condition ("each of the three vectors has at least one component \(\alpha_i\le 0.5\)") — the paper does not hide the confound, even if it does not directly resolve it.
- The mean-regret follow-up at lines 399-401 ("squared-distance's mean regret is \(\le 0.003\) in every asymmetric cell, while Manhattan's stays at 0.002–0.004") preserves the "Manhattan as hedge" reading consistently with the symmetric-grid story.
