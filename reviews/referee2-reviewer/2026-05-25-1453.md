## 1. Summary of the contribution

The paper studies frequency-report scoring rules — mechanisms that ask a subject to report a count vector \(r\in\Omega_n\) when realizations \(\omega\sim \mathrm{Mult}(n,p)\) are drawn — and asks what an observed \(r\) identifies about the subject's latent beliefs \(p\). The paper recasts that question as a partial-identification problem: each scoring rule \(S\) induces a mechanism-induced identified set \(P_S(r)=\{p:r\in R_S(p)\}\), from which coordinate and linear-functional bounds follow.

Three rules are compared:

1. **Squared-distance scoring** (the analytical centerpiece): Proposition 1 gives a closed-form linear identified set (a polytope in the simplex) and closed-form coordinate bounds; linear-functional bounds are linear programs over the same polytope.
2. **Frequency-guessing scoring** (Schlag and Tremewan, 2021, exact-match rule): closed-form coordinate bounds restated from the prior paper for self-containment.
3. **Manhattan-distance scoring**: exact identified set, sharp coordinate bounds via a one-dimensional binomial-CDF threshold root-find (semi-analytical), and grid-implemented mean bounds.

A structural lemma (single-transfer characterization) unifies the three under a separable resource-allocation problem with discrete-convex per-coordinate costs. The discussion takes up two further count losses — Hamming and Chebyshev — that violate the structural lemma's hypotheses and accordingly resist tractable sharp bounds for \(k>2\).

The contribution package, as stated:

- (N1) The partial-identification reframing of frequency-report elicitation.
- (N2) A unified three-rule comparison with a contextual recommendation (squared-distance for sparse beliefs, frequency-guessing for balanced beliefs, Manhattan as a regime-robust hedge).
- (N3) Proposition 1: the closed-form characterization of \(P_Q(r)\) and its coordinate bounds, plus Corollary 1's comparison of average coordinate widths.

The exercise is supported by a clean reproducible simulation grid (\(n\in\{5,10,20,50\}\), \(k\in\{2,3,5,10\}\), \(\alpha\in\{0.1,0.3,1,3,10\}\), 5,000 Dirichlet draws per cell, plus three asymmetric concentration vectors at \(n=20\), \(k=5\)).

## 2. The Berk question: "Would I have been pleased to write this paper?"

**Mostly, yes** — qualified by two reservations developed below.

The paper is a clean, careful, and self-aware methods note. The reproducibility infrastructure is unusually thorough for a paper of this scope (three independent `verify_regions.py` checks; a `consistency_check.py` that automatically reconciles paper tables against the underlying CSVs; full provenance to a seed). The mathematical content is correct (I spot-checked Proposition 1 and Lemma 1's proofs, and they go through). The prose is calibrated — when the paper hedges, it hedges in the right direction (e.g., "informativeness comparison is conditional on optimal reporting, not a behavioural prediction" at line 113 of \S5.3).

What I would *not* have been pleased to write, and what a referee will press the author on, is the **size of the analytical novelty**. Proposition 1 is the headline result. Strip away the partial-identification framing, and Proposition 1 says: "If you make a subject minimize \(\|r-np\|_2^2\) over feasible integer count vectors, then the report is the integer projection of the expected count vector onto the feasible set, and a single-transfer KKT calculation gives sharp linear coordinate bounds." This is correct, non-obvious enough to be useful, and not trivially derivable from prior work — but it is also a fairly direct application of an integer-projection argument that operations researchers and statisticians would consider routine. The paper's claim that "a single structural observation … organizes [the three characterizations]" (\S3.1) is true and elegant, but the observation itself — separable resource allocation with discrete-convex costs — is standard (the paper cites Fox 1966, Federgruen–Groenevelt 1986, Ibaraki–Katoh 1988, Murota 2003).

The contribution thus rests on **N1 (the reframing)** doing real work — i.e., the partial-identification lens must produce *new* understanding the prior literature would not have provided, beyond restating the optimal-report calculation. I am persuaded that it does, but the manuscript does not yet make the case that hardest. The audits have not pressed this point. A referee will.

## 3. Recommendation

**Minor revisions.**

The paper is in good shape and could plausibly be accepted at a methods-friendly venue with light revisions. But I cannot recommend acceptance as-is for the following reasons:

1. **M1 (abstract claim mismatch)** introduces an avoidable overclaim that a careful reader will catch in the abstract. This needs a one-paragraph fix.
2. **M2 (the Schlag–Tremewan boundary)** is the single most important question a referee will ask, and the manuscript's current answer is correct but incomplete. The paper needs one paragraph that makes the contribution-vs-prior-art line explicit.

These are tractable. Neither requires new analysis. Both involve sharpening claims that are already nearly right. I would not require new theorems, new simulations, or a re-architecting of the paper. I would, however, hold the paper to a higher bar than the prior audits did on these two points before stamping "accept" — because they are exactly the two questions an associate editor at a general-interest venue will weigh hardest.

## 4. Major comments

### M1. The abstract overclaims the Corollary 1 result; this is the single most visible defect a careful reader will catch on first pass.

**Location:** Abstract (main.tex line 42): "with a closed-form crossover in the number of positive-report coordinates, which a design comparison then locates as a crossover in the Dirichlet concentration parameter."

**Problem.** Corollary 1 establishes three things and only these:
- (1) \(W_0(n,k)\) is constant in \(r\);
- (2) \(W_Q(m;n,k)\) is a function of \(m\) alone;
- (3) \(W_Q(1;n,k) < W_0(n,k) < W_Q(k;n,k)\).

Part (3) proves the *existence* of a crossover \(m^\star\in\{2,\ldots,k-1\}\) (or \(k\) when there are gaps) such that \(W_Q(m^\star) \geq W_0\). It does not give \(m^\star\) in closed form. Computationally, for \((n,k)\in\{5,10,20,50\}\times\{3,5,10\}\), the crossover \(m^\star\) ranges from 3 (small \(k\)) to 9 (large \(k\), large \(n\)) and tracks roughly \((k+1)/2\) without an obvious closed-form solution.

The abstract's phrase "closed-form crossover" is therefore technically a closed-form *bracket* (i.e., \(W_Q(\cdot)\) is an explicit polynomial-rational function of \(m\), and so is \(W_0\)), but most readers will parse "closed-form crossover" as "a closed-form expression \(m^\star(n,k)\) where the widths are equal." A reader who turns to Corollary 1 to find this expression will not find one. This will frustrate a careful referee and will, more importantly, make them suspicious about whether the rest of the abstract is calibrated.

The same paragraph in \S1 (line 50) softens this correctly: "where the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths cross". That language is what the abstract should also use.

**What would change my mind.** Either of:
(a) Tighten the abstract to read "with the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths crossing as a function of the number of positive-report coordinates" (matching the \S1 phrasing); or
(b) State, prove, and display an actual closed-form expression for \(m^\star(n,k)\) — i.e., solve \(W_Q(m^\star;n,k) = W_0(n,k)\) explicitly. This is algebraically tractable (it is a polynomial equation in \(m\)), but the solution is not obviously instructive and (a) is the cleaner fix.

Severity. This is on the boundary between Major and Minor. I classify it Major because (i) it is in the abstract — the part of the paper most likely to be read carefully by an editor — and (ii) it touches a substantive claim about the analytical contribution. A casual reader will not notice; a careful one will, and they are precisely the readers whose verdicts matter for a general-interest tier.

### M2. The contribution boundary with Schlag and Tremewan (2021) is correct in substance but underdeveloped relative to what a referee will demand.

**Location:** \S1 lines 56–63 ("Related literature" first block); also \S3.2 line 183 ("These are the frequency-guessing bounds in Schlag and Tremewan, 2021, Proposition 1; they are restated here to make the informativeness comparison self-contained.").

**Problem.** Schlag and Tremewan (2021) already give, for the frequency-guessing rule:
- the mode characterization of optimal reports;
- the identified-set region of beliefs consistent with a mode report;
- coordinate bounds and bounds on means/variances via linear programs.

The paper acknowledges this honestly: "That inferential program, for this one rule, is theirs" (line 61). The paper's claim is that it carries the same program to *other* rules — squared-distance (with closed-form bounds) and Manhattan (with semi-analytical sharp bounds) — and turns the collection into a comparison.

A referee who has read Schlag and Tremewan will ask three sharper questions that the manuscript does not currently answer:

(a) **Do Schlag and Tremewan compare across rules?** The manuscript implies they do not. But Schlag and Tremewan also show that, for a binary event, frequency guessing is the *most precise rule eliciting a single frequency*. The paper notes this (line 62: "the question here is the different one of which rule is sharpest across belief regimes, where no rule wins outright"). But for a referee, the crucial point is the framing: their result is an *uniform dominance* claim within their class; the paper's result is a *contingent* claim about belief regimes. The contribution thus lives entirely in the gap between *those specific binary, single-frequency, exact-match* conditions and the present paper's *multinomial, multi-rule, regime-dependent* conditions. The manuscript should make this gap explicit in one sentence in the introduction. It currently does not.

(b) **What does the partial-ID reframing buy the reader that the Schlag–Tremewan framing does not?** Schlag and Tremewan get coordinate bounds via the same single-transfer inequalities. So the "identified set" language and the "partial identification" lens are, at a referee's first reading, terminology — a relabeling. The paper needs to argue more directly that the partial-ID lens *uses* something Schlag and Tremewan did not (e.g., produces bounds on linear functionals — means, variances, expected values — via a unified linear-program treatment; clarifies what "informativeness" formally means; or produces the regime crossover, which is genuinely new). The current introduction states this in passing (lines 50–53) but a referee at a general-interest venue will want it explicit and front-loaded.

(c) **How does the work relate to Schlag, Tremewan, and van der Weele (2015) — the SchlagEtAl2013 reference?** That paper surveys belief-elicitation methods broadly. Its relation to the present work is touched at lines 79–83 but not pinned down. A referee at a methods venue will care: does the present paper subsume part of their classification, complement it, or extend it?

**What would change my mind.** Add a short paragraph (3–5 sentences) immediately after the existing Schlag–Tremewan paragraph in the related-literature block, with these three sentences:

1. "Schlag and Tremewan's binary-event dominance result is uniform within a specific class; the present comparison is contingent across belief regimes and rules, and a uniform winner is shown not to exist."
2. "What the partial-identification framework adds beyond restated bounds is a unified treatment of linear-functional inference: mean and variance bounds on the same polytope (squared-distance, frequency-guessing) or threshold-indexed slice (Manhattan), without per-functional bespoke derivations."
3. "Schlag, Tremewan, and van der Weele (2015) survey elicitation methods broadly; this paper studies a specific within-method comparison — frequency-report rules under count losses — that their survey identifies as understudied."

If those three sentences are added, M2 dissolves. Without them, a referee will reasonably ask whether the contribution beyond Schlag and Tremewan is N1 (framing) doing real work or just a relabeling.

**Severity.** Major. This is the question every methods-section referee will pose first. If the answer is satisfactory, the paper is publishable; if it is not, the paper risks being read as "Schlag and Tremewan plus two more rules" — which is true but undersells the contribution.

## 5. Minor comments

### m1. \S3.1: the "structural lemma" framing is doing significant work — it deserves a one-sentence intuition before the proof.

The single-transfer characterization (Lemma 1) is the linchpin of the paper. \S3.1 begins with notation and \(\ell\)-functions before motivating the lemma. A reader who has not seen this in the resource-allocation literature will benefit from one sentence early in the subsection:

> "Intuition: if an optimal report cannot be improved by moving one count from any positive category to any other, then under discrete convexity it cannot be improved by any larger transfer either, because each successive count transfer along a path to a competing report is at least as costly. Discrete convexity is what makes the local condition global."

This is what Federgruen–Groenevelt (1986) state in their classical paper, and a one-line restatement here would frame the formalism. The proof in Appendix A is fine; the prose in \S3.1 is currently terse.

### m2. \S5.3: the "centre-bias → squared-distance moves continuously" claim is correct but the prose underplays its strongest version.

Line 116: "This moves the squared-distance identified set continuously, because that set is the projection polytope of the reported count vector and shifts smoothly with the reported coordinates."

The substance is right — Proposition 1 makes \(P_Q(r)\) the projection polytope and so a small shift in \(r\) shifts the polytope smoothly. But "smoothly" is the wrong word for a polytope shifting by integer count transfers in \(r\). The shift is *piecewise linear in the continuous embedding* (i.e., if you allow fractional \(r\), the polytope vertices move linearly) but *discrete* as \(r\) ranges over \(\Omega_n\). A referee with a partial-ID background will read "smoothly" and pause.

**Fix.** Replace "shifts smoothly with the reported coordinates" with "shifts by a single facet-translation when one count transfers between categories, while the frequency-guessing identified set can jump between mode cells with the same continuity in \(r\)" — or similar. The contrast is between *facet-translation* (squared-distance) and *cell-jump* (frequency-guessing), which is sharper than "smooth vs. discrete."

### m3. \S4: the 5,000-draw simulation has no reported standard errors on the win shares.

The win-share table (Table 1 in the paper, `tab:regime-wins`) reports values like 0.84 and 0.58 to two decimal places. With 5,000 draws per cell, the standard error of a Bernoulli win-share is at most \(\sqrt{0.5\cdot 0.5/5000} \approx 0.007\) — so two decimal places is well within precision. But the *averaging across cells* (5 \(n\) values \(\times\) 4 \(k\) values = 20 cells, each with 5,000 draws) averages over correlated outcomes in a way the paper does not document. A referee will not stop the paper for this, but a one-line footnote in \S4 confirming that the reported values are sample averages over \(5000 \cdot |n\text{-grid}| \cdot |k\text{-grid}| = 100{,}000\) draws would close the issue cleanly.

### m4. The exposition of Manhattan mean bounds is harder than necessary.

Section 3.4, paragraph "Identification bounds." (lines 266–278) explains the threshold representation in the most general form first. For a referee unfamiliar with the binomial-CDF inversion, it would help to give the \(k=2\) case (lines 267–271) as a fully worked example with a numerical illustration — e.g., "for \(n=10\), report \((6,4)\), the binomial-median interval for \(p_1\) is approximately \([0.51, 0.69]\)" — before generalizing. This is exposition-only; no claim changes.

## 6. Specific questions for the author

The following are the questions I would ask in a seminar Q&A:

1. **Why frequency reports over standard probability reports at all?** \S1 invokes Gigerenzer–Hoffrage (1995) for the cognitive premise but does not test it. Beyond that citation, what is the strongest available evidence that the cognitive advantage is real *for the populations and decision tasks experimentalists actually run*? Hogarth (1975) is a 50-year-old reference; Gigerenzer–Hoffrage's frequency-format finding is for *Bayesian-reasoning* tasks, not belief-elicitation tasks per se. Will an editor be satisfied that the case for frequency reports rests on a "behavioural premise this paper does not test" (\S5.3 line 109)?

2. **Crossover at $\alpha=1$ is suspicious.** The crossover from squared-distance dominance to frequency-guessing dominance happens "near \(\alpha=1\)" (\S4 line 52). The symmetric Dirichlet at \(\alpha=1\) is the *uniform distribution* on the simplex. Is the crossover a generic phenomenon, or an artifact of the symmetric Dirichlet's specific concentration interpretation? The asymmetric robustness (Table 3 in the appendix) is helpful but uses three vectors all containing at least one small component (the domain-reviewer flagged this as AB1). What happens at, say, \(\boldsymbol\alpha=(5,4,3,2,2)\) — all components \(\geq 2\), no small components, asymmetric? Does squared-distance still dominate, or does frequency-guessing take over?

3. **Manhattan median-orientation vs. squared-distance mean-orientation: where does this matter substantively?** \S3.4 connects Manhattan to absolute-error loss eliciting the median (Gneiting 2011). But the design comparison uses an *outcome vector with linear-mean target* — i.e., an objective that is exactly what squared-distance is built for. Is the design comparison giving squared-distance an unfair home-field advantage? What would happen if the inferential target were a *median* or *quantile* of the belief distribution? Section 4 says "the ranking is robust to the choice of outcome vector" (line 57), checking only the extreme-category vector vs. the evenly spaced vector — but both are linear functionals. A nonlinear target would test the median-orientation claim.

4. **What can a researcher say about \(p\) at \(n=1\)?** The most interesting limit case for an experimentalist is \(n=1\) — a single-trial elicitation, which is the standard belief-elicitation format. The paper studies \(n \geq 2\) (Corollary 1 requires \(n\geq 2\)). What collapses, or what becomes uninformative, at \(n=1\)? At \(n=1\) only one category gets a positive count (the report is \(e_i\) for some \(i\)), so \(m(r)=1\) and the squared-distance and frequency-guessing rules might or might not coincide. A clarifying remark would help readers understand whether the paper's frame includes single-trial elicitation as a degenerate case.

5. **What is the Schlag–Tremewan inferential program's relation to your linear-functional bounds?** Schlag and Tremewan (2021) derive bounds on individual probabilities and on means and variances via linear programs. Your Proposition 1 gives the corresponding closed-form individual-probability bounds for squared-distance and notes that means follow as linear programs. Are the linear programs you solve for means and variances the same machine Schlag and Tremewan deploy for frequency-guessing — i.e., LP over a polytope of inequalities derived from single-transfer optimality — or different? If they are the *same machine* applied to a different rule, then the partial-identification framing is the key conceptual unification; if they are *different machines*, then there is additional methodological contribution worth flagging.

6. **The "no rule is universally best" message is the headline. But is the message robust to risk-averse subjects?** The body assumes risk neutrality (\S2 line 72). The discussion (\S5.1) shows that binary-lottery implementation extends to risk-averse EU subjects. But under DVW (2022) the binary-lottery implementation introduces *centre-bias*, which (the new \S5.3 paragraph argues) affects squared-distance more than frequency-guessing. If centre-bias is real and the binary-lottery implementation is what brings the distance rules into risk-aversion-robust territory, then the practical recommendation "use squared-distance when beliefs are sparse" depends on subjects not centre-biasing — which is exactly the condition DVW found can fail. Is the headline recommendation robust to a published behavioural failure of its required implementation?

## 7. The conditional on optimal reporting (point 4 in the brief)

The \S5.3 paragraph "An Open Empirical Question" handles this honestly. Three observations:

- The paragraph is correctly hedged ("predictions, not assertions") and the four sub-claims it makes (centre-bias, hedging, lottery-confusion, and induced-beliefs designs) are individually defensible — the domain-reviewer has already verified this.
- The framing is unusual in a positive way: rather than treating the optimal-reporting conditional as a limitation, the paper *uses partial identification* to turn it into a set of testable predictions. That is a real contribution and one a referee will note approvingly.
- That said, a referee at a methods-friendly venue may push for *one* of the predictions to be sketched in slightly more depth. For example: under centre-biased reporting, what does the squared-distance identified set look like, and does it still contain the true \(p\)? Even a one-paragraph thought experiment (no new theorem, no new simulation) would strengthen the claim that "the partial-identification framework itself, however, suggests how both premises could be examined."

**Verdict on conditional handling.** Acceptable. The paragraph does what a methods note should do — acknowledges the limitation, maps it to the per-rule mechanism, and proposes a design that would test it. This is better than the audits gave it credit for; it is also not so far developed that a referee would consider it a substitute for actual behavioural testing.

## 8. The three-rule choice and Hamming exclusion (point 5 in the brief)

\S5.2 ("Other Frequency-Report Scoring Rules and the Limits of the Approach") handles this defensibly:

- The Hamming exclusion is now framed as *structural*: Hamming's per-coordinate cost violates Lemma 1's discrete-convexity hypothesis, and the failure is *interior*, not boundary (the worked counter-example at \(n=3, k=5\), uniform beliefs). This is the right way to frame the exclusion. It is not "we tried and it was hard"; it is "Hamming is not in the tractable family the lemma identifies."
- The modal-box inner bound and the polynomial-inequality outer bound are stated. The combinatorial blowup at \(n=50, k=10\) is documented (\(>10^{10}\) constraints).
- Chebyshev's non-separability is the second failure mode of Lemma 1.

A skeptical referee will still ask:

- **Why not a smaller Hamming grid?** Hamming is computable for small \(n\), \(k\). The paper could present Hamming results on the \(n\in\{5,10\}\), \(k\in\{2,3,5\}\) subgrid where it is tractable, and explicitly note that Hamming is excluded *for the large cells only*. As it stands, Hamming is excluded entirely. A 12-cell Hamming subtable would close this loophole at modest cost.
- **Is the modal-box inner bound informative?** The closed-form inner bound \(\prod_i[r_i/(n+1),(r_i+1)/(n+1)]\) is presented but its width is not compared to the other rules. A one-sentence width comparison would tell the reader whether the inner bound is competitive or vacuous.

**Verdict on Hamming exclusion.** Defensible. The structural framing (Lemma 1's hypothesis failure) is the right one. A small Hamming subtable would close the only obvious loophole.

## 9. Practical relevance (point 6 in the brief): why frequency reports?

The paper invokes Gigerenzer–Hoffrage (1995) for the cognitive premise and Hogarth (1975) for the historical motivation. These are honest citations, but they are not specific tests of frequency-report scoring rules in belief-elicitation contexts.

The strongest case the paper makes for frequency reports is *operational*, not cognitive: the count format produces tractable identified sets, while continuous probability reports do not (or require different machinery). This is the contribution; the cognitive claim is a side benefit the paper does not test.

A referee may push: **the paper would be stronger if it stated the practical case as "frequency reports are operationally tractable for inferring identified sets, and possibly cognitively simpler for subjects, but the cognitive claim is taken on the cited literature."** This is roughly what \S5.3 says, but the introduction at \S1 lines 6–8 is more committed to the cognitive claim than the rest of the paper.

**Verdict on practical relevance.** Adequate, with a small mismatch between \S1 (committed to the cognitive claim) and \S5.3 (hedged on the cognitive claim). One-sentence reconciliation would help.
