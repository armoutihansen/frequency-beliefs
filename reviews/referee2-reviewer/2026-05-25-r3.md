## 1. Was the §3.6 expansion the right move?

Yes. Three reasons.

**1.1 It earns the abstract's promise.** The abstract sentence at `main.tex:41` ("For each rule the same identified set yields bounds on variance, median, and quantile functionals of the induced outcome distribution via the same linear, quadratic, or feasibility programs over a single underlying region") was added simultaneously with §3.6. Without §3.6, that abstract sentence would be an unsupported promise. With §3.6, it is now the headline of a delivered result.

**1.2 It markets the contribution correctly.** The framework's central virtue is *unification* — one identified set per rule, one optimization machine, every functional. Before §3.6, the paper sold "coordinate + mean bounds via a polytope LP". After §3.6, it sells "every functional of the induced outcome distribution via the same optimization machine." This is a strictly larger statement of contribution, supported by a worked example that displays five functionals (coordinate, mean, median, lower quartile, upper quartile) for three rules on a single table. The cost — two pages, one lemma, one table — is small. The benefit — converting a methods note into a methods note with a clean inferential menu — is large.

**1.3 It absorbs the Gneiting non-reflection cleanly.** A natural reader objection would be "if Manhattan elicits the median in the standard scoring-rule sense, surely it gives the tightest median bounds?" The worked example shows it doesn't — frequency-guessing gives tighter median bounds at the symmetric report. §3.6 line 340 makes the distinction explicit: "The Gneiting (2011) correspondence between absolute-error loss and the median, invoked in Section 3.4 at the level of optimal reports, is a statement about *elicitation*, not *identified-set tightness*." This is the right framing. Without §3.6, the same potential confusion would persist in the reader's head; with §3.6, it is named and resolved on the same page.

The alternative — deferring the menu to a follow-up paper — would have left the abstract's claim unsupported and the Gneiting confusion latent. The §3.6 expansion was the right call.

## 2. Is the Lemma `lem:l1-linear` statement and proof journal-grade?

Yes. I checked the statement-proof match line by line.

**Statement (§3.6 lines 304–315).** Claims, for any \(a\in\R^k\):
\[
\sup_{p\in P_M(r)} \textstyle\sum_i a_i p_i = \sup_{c\in[0,1]} \sup\{\textstyle\sum_i a_i p_i : p_v^{(L)}(c)\le p_v\le p_v^{(U)}(c)\,\forall v,\ \sum_v p_v=1\}.
\]
Then specialises to indicator vectors \(a=(\1\{i\le j\})\) for cumulative-sum bounds, then to \(\tau\)-quantile-index bounds via the displayed identities.

**Proof (`08_appendix.tex:323–340`).** Establishes (a) \(P_M(r)=\bigcup_{c\in[0,1]}\Sigma(c)\) from Proposition `prop:manhattan` (already proved); (b) sup-over-union equals sup-of-sups (elementary); (c) the inner problem at fixed \(c\) is an LP over the box-simplex slice (by inspection); (d) cumulative-sum specialisation by direct substitution \(a_i=\1\{i\le j\}\); (e) the quantile-index identities from \(j^*_\tau(p)\le j\) iff \(S_j(p)\ge\tau\) and \(j^*_\tau(p)\ge j\) iff \(S_{j-1}(p)<\tau\), both immediate from \(j^*_\tau(p)=\min\{j:S_j(p)\ge\tau\}\) and monotonicity of \(S_j\) in \(j\).

Every claim in the statement is matched in the proof. No silent appeals. The empty-slice convention (`-infty`) is stated. The cross-reference to `lem:manhattan-unimodal` for the endpoint definitions is appropriate. The proof is short — eight printed lines — but that brevity is honest: the lemma is, structurally, the sup-of-union identity plus the cumulative-sum and quantile bookkeeping, none of which requires more than that. A longer proof would inflate this without adding content.

**Statement-side polish.** The lemma writes `sup_{p in P_M(r)}` throughout, but `P_M(r)` is closed and bounded, so the sup is attained — strictly, `max` would be more conventional. The convention "sup with `-infty` when the slice is empty" is needed for the inner problem (some `c` values yield an empty box-simplex intersection), so the outer `sup` is genuinely a `sup` only because some slices contribute `-infty`. The current notation is internally consistent and the convention is stated. No revision needed.

**One small statement-side improvement** (optional). The quantile-index identity is written as
\[
\min_p j^*_\tau(p)=\min\{j:\sup_p S_j(p)\ge\tau\},\qquad \max_p j^*_\tau(p)=\max\{j:\inf_p S_{j-1}(p)<\tau\},
\]
with `p` ranging over `P_M(r)` understood from context. Adding the `p \in P_M(r)` to the second `sup`/`inf` (so all four optimizations have explicit feasible sets) would forestall any reader confusion. This is a one-symbol edit, not a defect.

**Verdict on lemma.** Journal-grade.

## 3. (Major) Variance direction inverted in §3.6 and Table caption

This is the new substantive issue.

**Where.** §3.6 line 300, and Table `tab:functional-menu` caption line 336.

**Quote (line 300).** "[...] quadratic \(T\) (variance) to a quadratic program---convex for the lower bound and a non-convex maximum over the polytope vertices for the upper bound."

**Quote (caption line 336).** "Variance bounds follow from a constrained optimization over the same identified sets (a convex quadratic program for the lower bound, a vertex search for the upper) [...]"

**Why it's wrong.** For \(Y=x_J\) with \(J\sim p\),
\[
\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \textstyle\sum_i x_i^2 p_i - \big(\sum_i x_i p_i\big)^2 = c^\top p - p^\top (xx^\top) p,
\]
with \(c_i=x_i^2\). The Hessian with respect to \(p\) is \(\nabla^2\mathrm{Var}(Y) = -2xx^\top\), which is **negative semidefinite** (rank-1, with the single nonzero eigenvalue equal to \(-2\|x\|^2\)). Therefore \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) is a **concave** function of \(p\). I verified this numerically: for \(x=(0,1,2,3,4)\) the nonzero eigenvalue of the Hessian is \(-60\), confirming concavity.

Concave optimization over a convex polytope has the *reverse* min/max structure from a convex objective:

- **Maximum of concave** = a convex program (minimize the convex function \(-\mathrm{Var}\)). The maximizer is generically interior to the polytope.
- **Minimum of concave** = a non-convex program; the minimum of a concave function over a convex compact set is attained at an extreme point (vertex).

So the **upper** variance bound is the convex QP, and the **lower** variance bound is the vertex-search/branch-and-bound problem. The paper has the directions reversed.

**Sanity check.** I ran a random-feasibility search on the frequency-guessing polytope for \(n=10\), \(k=5\), \(r=(2,2,2,2,2)\), \(x=(0,1,2,3,4)\):

| | Empirical |
|---|---|
| min \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) over polytope | 1.80 |
| max \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) over polytope | 2.24 |
| \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) at uniform \(p=(0.2,\ldots,0.2)\) (interior point) | 2.00 |

The uniform point is interior to the polytope and gives a *large* variance — close to but not equal to the empirical maximum. A more thorough convex-QP solve from a feasible starting point would find the interior maximizer. The minimum, by contrast, will be attained at one of the polytope's vertices (the extreme points of the partial-identification region). This pattern — max attained interior, min attained at a vertex — is the *opposite* of what the §3.6 prose and caption describe.

**Why this matters.** Variance bounds are a *flagship* partial-identification functional. Any active researcher in the area (e.g., Manski-style partial-identification, Tamer-style set inference, Schlag–Tremewan's own variance bounds for the frequency-guessing rule) will know on sight that the variance of a Bernoulli-mixture is concave in the mixing weights. A reader who is not familiar with the area will likely not catch this. But the journal's referee pool for a methods-section paper on partial identification of belief functionals is precisely the population that will catch it. This is the most catchable kind of error: a sign or direction inversion in a flagship example, in a section that is otherwise correct, in a paper whose central claim is a unified optimization machine.

**What would change my mind.** A two-edit fix. (a) §3.6 line 300: swap "convex for the lower bound and a non-convex maximum over the polytope vertices for the upper bound" to "convex for the *upper* bound and a non-convex *minimum* over the polytope vertices for the *lower* bound (since \(\mathrm{Var}(Y)\) is concave in \(p\), with Hessian \(-2xx^\top\))". (b) Caption line 336: swap "convex quadratic program for the lower bound, a vertex search for the upper" to "convex quadratic program for the upper bound, a vertex search for the lower". Optionally, a one-clause justification of concavity in the prose ("variance is concave in \(p\) because \(\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \sum_i x_i^2 p_i - (\sum_i x_i p_i)^2\) has Hessian \(-2xx^\top\)") would head off the question entirely.

Note that the *abstract* sentence at line 41 — "via the same linear, quadratic, or feasibility programs over a single underlying region" — does *not* commit to a direction and is therefore not affected by this error. Only the §3.6 elaboration and the Table 4 caption need editing.

The code-reviewer's r4 report (`reviews/code-reviewer/2026-05-25-r4.md`) separately observes that variance bounds are *mentioned* but no code path computes them — its recommendation is "Either add a variance-bound routine matching the §3.6 caption, or soften that caption." Combining the code reviewer's observation with the math error: the cleanest fix is to either (i) add the routine *and* fix the direction in the caption, or (ii) drop the variance mention from §3.6 entirely and keep only mean / median / quantile in the worked example, deferring variance to a follow-up. Option (ii) is the least intrusive; option (i) is the more complete delivery. The author's call.

## 5. Framing of the Gneiting non-reflection — verified

§3.6 line 340 reads: "The Gneiting (2011) correspondence between absolute-error loss and the median, invoked in Section 3.4 at the level of optimal reports, is a statement about *elicitation*, not *identified-set tightness*: at the symmetric report displayed in Table `tab:functional-menu` the median-eliciting Manhattan rule does not give tighter median bounds than frequency-guessing."

This is exactly the right framing. The Gneiting (2011) classical result establishes that absolute-error loss elicits the *median* in the sense that the median is the *optimal point forecast* under absolute-error loss. That is a statement about which `r` is induced by which `p` — the *forward* map from beliefs to optimal reports. The current paper studies the *inverse* map — what does an observed report identify about beliefs — and the inferential menu is then defined on the resulting identified sets. There is no theorem that says "the rule that elicits T as the optimal report also gives the tightest identified-set bound on T." The §3.6 example shows that on a particular symmetric report, the median-eliciting rule does not give the tightest median bounds. The paper's framing is conservative and precise.

I also confirm that:

- The text does not generalize from one example to a universal claim ("Manhattan never gives the tightest median bounds" would be unsupported and overclaiming). The current text is specifically scoped to "at the symmetric report displayed in Table `tab:functional-menu`".
- The text properly attributes the existing literature: "\citet{SchlagTremewan2021} derive bounds on means, variances, and quantiles for frequency-guessing in their setting; the statement above carries that program across all three rules." This is the right credit — the contribution is the carryover across rules, not the existence of LP-over-polytope bounds for one rule.

This is one of the cleanest pieces of academic-writing self-discipline in the paper.

## 6. (Minor) "Sequence of feasibility linear programs" wording

§3.6 line 300 describes the quantile reduction as "a sequence of feasibility linear programs, one per candidate value, asking whether some belief in the polytope attains it." This is correct but cleaner if matched to the lemma's actual statement: it is `O(k)` *optimization* LPs computing \(\sup_p S_j(p)\) and \(\inf_p S_{j-1}(p)\) for \(j=1,\ldots,k\), and the min/max quantile-index identities then pick out the right `j`. A feasibility LP and an optimization LP are computationally interchangeable here, so this is a presentation choice. The lemma writes the optimization version, the §3.6 prose writes the feasibility version. They are equivalent, and a reader who reads both will figure that out, but the §3.6 prose would be marginally smoother if it said "a sequence of cumulative-sum optimization LPs over the polytope (Lemma~\ref{lem:l1-linear})" or similar.

This is a presentation suggestion, not a defect.

## 7. (Line-edit) Hamming absence from the inferential menu

The code reviewer flagged that the §3.6 worked-example table shows three rules — frequency-guessing, squared-distance, Manhattan — and omits Hamming, which is otherwise discussed in the paper as one of the four "headline" rules in earlier framing. The omission is structurally correct: §3.6's machinery applies only when the identified set is either a polytope (squared, frequency-guessing) or a tractable union of box-simplex slices (Manhattan); Hamming distance fails both. A one-sentence footnote at the start of §3.6 or in the table caption clarifying this would prevent a reader confused by the rule-count change from missing the point. Optional.
