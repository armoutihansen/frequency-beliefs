## 1. Verification that M1 and M2 are genuinely resolved

### M1 (abstract overclaim) — RESOLVED

**Round 1 demand.** Either (a) tighten the abstract to match §1's "where the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths cross" phrasing, or (b) state and display a closed-form expression for the crossover \(m^\star(n,k)\).

**Author's choice.** Option (a). Abstract line 42 now reads "with the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths crossing as a function of the number of positive-report coordinates---a crossover a design comparison then locates in the Dirichlet concentration parameter."

**Verification.** Compared with §1 line 50, "where the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths cross", the abstract phrasing is now consistent. The word "closed-form" no longer modifies "crossover"; it modifies "rules", which is what Corollary 1 actually proves. The two-stage structure — the analytical existence of the crossover, then its empirical location in the Dirichlet concentration parameter — is communicated cleanly. **A careful editor reading abstract-then-Corollary-1 will not now find a mismatch.**

One small remaining tightness consideration. The phrase "with the two closed-form rules' average coordinate widths crossing as a function of the number of positive-report coordinates" is precise but slightly heavy for an abstract. This is a stylistic note, not a defect; I would not require a further edit.

### M2 (Schlag–Tremewan boundary) — RESOLVED

**Round 1 demand.** Three sentences after the existing Schlag–Tremewan paragraph in the related-literature block: (1) uniform-within-class vs. contingent-across-regimes; (2) what the partial-ID lens specifically buys (linear-functional unification, not relabelling); (3) the relation to Schlag–Tremewan–van der Weele (2015).

**Author's choice.** Three sentences added at §1 lines 64–67, framed as a "three-fold" boundary:

1. *(Uniform vs. contingent.)* "Their binary-event dominance result is uniform within their class of single-frequency-eliciting rules; the present comparison is contingent across multinomial belief regimes and rules, and a uniform winner is shown not to exist." This is precise and substantively correct. The framing — uniform-within-class vs. contingent-across-regimes — is exactly what a methods-section referee will look for.
2. *(What partial-ID adds.)* "What the partial-identification framework adds beyond restated bounds is a unified treatment of linear-functional inference: mean and linear-functional bounds on a single polytope of single-transfer inequalities for the closed-form rules, and on a single threshold-indexed box-simplex slice for Manhattan, without per-functional bespoke derivations." This is the right answer to the "is N1 doing work or just relabelling?" question, and it is grounded in the manuscript's actual contribution (the LP-over-polytope unification across rules).
3. *(SchlagEtAl2013 survey position.)* "\citet{SchlagEtAl2013} survey belief-elicitation methods broadly; the present paper studies a specific within-method comparison---frequency-report rules under count losses---that the survey identifies as understudied." Cleanly delineates broad-survey-vs-specific-within-method positioning. I confirmed `SchlagEtAl2013` is in `references.bib` as Schlag, Tremewan, and van der Weele (line 41–42 of the .bib).

**Verification.** All three demands satisfied as specified. The paragraph integrates with the surrounding related-literature block without disrupting the flow.

**Caveat (minor, not a regression).** Sentence 2 makes the claim that the partial-ID framework adds "linear-functional inference" beyond what Schlag–Tremewan derive. This is *true on net* — the squared-distance and Manhattan linear-functional bounds are not in Schlag–Tremewan, and Proposition 1's polytope is new. But Schlag–Tremewan (2021) *do* derive mean and variance bounds for the frequency-guessing rule via LPs over the mode polytope. Strictly, then, what the present paper adds is not "linear-functional inference for the family" — it is *the carryover of the LP-over-polytope machine to the other two rules*. The current wording can be read either way; a hostile referee might press the point. I would tweak sentence 2 in a final pass to read: "What the partial-identification framework adds beyond restated bounds is a unified treatment of linear-functional inference *across rules*: the same single-transfer-polytope LP for both closed-form rules and a threshold-indexed extension for Manhattan, rather than per-rule bespoke derivations." This is a one-word fix and not a revision-worthy change.

## 2. Verification of the four-cell robustness table (Q2 / domain-reviewer AB1)

**Round 1 question.** What happens at \(\boldsymbol\alpha=(5,4,3,2,2)\) — all components \(\geq 2\), no small components, asymmetric? Does squared-distance still dominate, or does frequency-guessing take over?

**Author's choice.** Added the fourth asymmetric cell. Win shares for coord_avg are 0.84 / 0.11 / 0.05 (frequency-guessing / squared / Manhattan) and for mean_linear 0.68 / 0.27 / 0.05. The ranking **flips**: frequency-guessing dominates the balanced-asymmetric case. Appendix Table 3 is reorganized into "Concentrated-asymmetric (at least one \(\alpha_i\le 0.5\))" and "Balanced-asymmetric (all \(\alpha_i\ge 2\))" subgroups.

**Verification of the table itself.** I read appendix lines 376–406 carefully. The structural framing — "the regime crossover therefore reproduces under asymmetry exactly as in the symmetric grid: when the resulting beliefs are marginally concentrated, squared-distance wins; when they are balanced, frequency-guessing wins. The governing variable is the marginal concentration of the draws, not the symmetry of the Dirichlet" — is exactly right. The new fourth cell is a strong robustness signal because it confirms a *prediction* rather than just adding noise: the prediction was that "marginal concentration" governs the crossover, not "Dirichlet symmetry", and the fourth cell tests that prediction by holding asymmetry and *removing* small components, then watching the ranking flip in the predicted direction.

**Verification of the §4 pointer.** §4 line 57 reads: "the ranking is robust to the choice of outcome vector and is unaffected by optimal-report ties [...], along with four asymmetric concentration vectors at \(n=20\), \(k=5\) (Table~\ref{tab:regime-wins-asymmetric}) for which the regime crossover reproduces under asymmetry exactly as in the symmetric grid: squared-distance dominates the three concentrated-asymmetric cells (with at least one component \(\alpha_i\le 0.5\)) and frequency-guessing dominates the balanced-asymmetric cell (all components \(\ge 2\))." Then line 58: "The governing variable is the marginal concentration of the resulting beliefs, not the symmetry of the Dirichlet itself." This is the strongest available framing of the new evidence. The flip is *named* (not buried) and the mechanism (marginal concentration as the governing variable) is stated explicitly. **This is the right way to present a robustness check whose result is "the qualitative prediction holds, and the quantitative ranking flips in the predicted direction."**

**No overclaiming.** I checked specifically for the kinds of overclaims that would be tempting here:

- The text does NOT say "the asymmetric robustness check confirms squared-distance dominance" (which would have been false for the fourth cell).
- The text does NOT say "the crossover happens at any belief asymmetry" (which would have been an unwarranted generalization from four cells).
- The text DOES say "the governing variable is the marginal concentration of the resulting beliefs, not the symmetry of the Dirichlet itself" (correct, supported, and important).
- The text DOES retain "the regime crossover therefore reproduces under asymmetry" (correct: three cells where squared wins, one where guessing wins — both regimes are present).

The framing is calibrated to the evidence. This is a clear example of the author absorbing a critical AB1 finding and turning it from a potential weakness into a *strengthening* of the headline claim (because the mechanism — marginal concentration — is now explicit rather than implicit).

## 3. Numerical verification of the new worked example (m4)

The paper now reads, at §3.4 line 272: "for \(n=10\) and the report \(r=(6,4)\), inverting the binomial CDF at \(1/2\) at the integer boundaries \(t-1=5\) and \(t=6\) gives \(P_{M,1}(r)\approx[0.55, 0.64]\)---the set of beliefs at which 6 is a median of \(\mathrm{Bin}(10,p_1)\)."

**Independent numerical check (using scipy.stats.binom.cdf and brentq):**

```
F(5;10,p) = 0.5  ⇒  p = 0.5483
F(6;10,p) = 0.5  ⇒  p = 0.6449
Identified set P_{M,1}((6,4)) = [0.5483, 0.6449]
```

- At \(p=0.55\): \(F(5)=0.4956\le0.5\le0.7340=F(6)\) ✓ (6 is a median).
- At \(p=0.64\): \(F(5)=0.2708\le0.5\le0.5132=F(6)\) ✓ (6 is a median).
- At \(p=0.50\): \(F(5)=0.6230\) > 0.5, so 6 is *not* a median (median is 5). ✓ matches the claim that 0.55 is the left endpoint.
- At \(p=0.65\): \(F(6)=0.4862\) < 0.5, so 6 is *not* a median (median is 7). ✓ matches the claim that 0.64 is the right endpoint.

The displayed interval [0.55, 0.64] is the true interval [0.5483, 0.6449] **rounded inward** (i.e., the displayed interval is a strict subset of the true identified set). This rounding direction is conservative — the paper *under*states the precision of the identified set, not over. Given that the paper uses "\(\approx\)", inward rounding is the correct convention. No revision needed.

### 4.3 §3.1 line 22 (Lemma 1 intuition)

The new sentence: "The intuition for what follows is that, under discrete convexity, no local single-count transfer being profitable rules out any larger transfer too: each successive count moved along a path to a competing report is at least as costly as the first, so the local no-improvement condition is also the global one."

This is exactly the verbal description of the monotone-path exchange argument that the proof in Appendix A executes formally. It frames Lemma 1 *before* the notation hits the reader. The phrase "successive count moved along a path to a competing report is at least as costly as the first" is the right mechanism description: discrete convexity gives nondecreasing differences, which is what makes the exchange argument go through. **No defect.** The constructive pet peeve "mechanism-level intuition before formalism" is now satisfied.

### 4.4 §7 line 116 ("smoothly" replacement)

The new sentence: "This moves the squared-distance identified set by a single facet-translation when one count transfers between categories, because that set is the projection polytope of the reported count vector; the frequency-guessing identified set, by contrast, can jump between mode cells with the same single-count change, so a range of beliefs can produce the same identified set."

This replaces "shifts smoothly with the reported coordinates", which I flagged in Round 1 as imprecise for integer-valued \(r\). The new wording draws the contrast between *facet-translation* (squared-distance) and *cell-jump* (frequency-guessing) — exactly the right structural framing for a partial-ID-trained reader. **No defect.**

The clause "so a range of beliefs can produce the same identified set" applies to *both* rules (the identified set is by construction a region of beliefs); a fastidious reader could ask whether the clause is meant to characterize specifically frequency-guessing's cell structure (where multiple modes share a region). It does — the cell-jump structure means a small shift in \(r\) can move from one mode cell to another, leaving the identified set unchanged within each cell. This is sound, but the sentence reads as if the "range of beliefs" point is novel to frequency-guessing when it is in fact a general property. **Minor presentation issue, not a defect** — see m1-R2 below.

### 4.5 §4 line 30 footnote (m3)

The new footnote: "Each row of Table~\ref{tab:regime-wins} averages over \(5{,}000\) draws per cell and over the \(4\times 4\) \((n,k)\) grid, so \(80{,}000\) draws per row; the binomial standard error on a single-rule win share is at most \(\sqrt{0.25/80{,}000}\approx 0.0018\), well under the two displayed decimals."

This is the correct calculation: \(\sqrt{0.25/80000} = 0.00177\). The "binomial standard error" framing assumes independent Bernoulli win indicators, which is reasonable given the independent Dirichlet draws across cells. Two-decimal precision is well clear of the SE. **No defect.** Note: I had said in Round 1 that this was \(\sqrt{0.5\cdot0.5/5000}\approx 0.007\) per *cell*; the footnote correctly aggregates across the (n,k) grid, giving 80,000 draws per row and the smaller 0.0018 SE. The author's calculation is more accurate than mine.

### 5.2 Two Minor presentation issues survive self-challenge.

**m1-R2.** §7 line 116 (the facet-translation vs. cell-jump contrast). The clause "so a range of beliefs can produce the same identified set" is correct but applies to both rules (this is what an identified set *is*). The intended meaning is that under frequency-guessing, the *cell structure* of the mode polytope makes a *family* of reports correspond to the same mode region, whereas under squared-distance, each report has a *distinct* projection polytope. The intended contrast is sharper than the current sentence delivers.

**Suggested fix (line-edit, not revision-worthy):** "the frequency-guessing identified set, by contrast, can jump between mode cells with the same single-count change, so different reports can correspond to the same mode region."

**m2-R2.** §1 line 66 (sentence 2 of the new Schlag boundary block). As noted in §1 of this report, the sentence currently reads "the partial-identification framework adds beyond restated bounds is a unified treatment of linear-functional inference". A hostile referee could read this as denying that Schlag–Tremewan derive linear-functional bounds for their rule, which they do. The intended meaning is "carryover of the LP-over-polytope machine to *other rules*", not "linear-functional bounds did not previously exist". A one-clause edit (insert "across rules" or "for the family") would close this.

**Suggested fix (line-edit):** "What the partial-identification framework adds beyond restated bounds is a unified treatment of linear-functional inference *across rules*: the same single-transfer-polytope LP for both closed-form rules and a threshold-indexed extension for Manhattan, rather than per-rule bespoke derivations."

Neither of these is a revision request. Both are copy-edit suggestions for the final proofs.

### 5.3 One question carried to response letter (not blocking)

Round-1 Question 4 (what collapses at \(n=1\)?) remains worth a sentence in §2 or §3 if the author has space, but it is not blocking. At \(n=1\) the report is a one-hot vector \(e_i\), so \(m(r)=1\) and Corollary 1's squared-distance width formula reads \(W_Q(1;n=1,k) = (1/k)\cdot[2-1-1/k+(k-1)/2] = (1/k)\cdot(1-1/k+(k-1)/2)\). The structure of the squared-distance bounds at \(n=1\) is a useful sanity-check the author could mention in a footnote at §3.2. Not blocking.
