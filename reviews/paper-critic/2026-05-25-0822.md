## Contribution Assessment

This is a careful, well-organized methodological note: it recasts incentivized frequency-report belief elicitation as a partial-identification problem and compares three count-loss rules (squared-distance, frequency-guessing, Manhattan) by the informativeness of the identified set each report induces, with Hamming/Chebyshev relegated to a boundary discussion. The Schlag–Tremewan prior work is attributed transparently and the "no universally best rule" framing is honest. The contribution is genuine but modest in scope (a unified partial-identification framing plus the squared-distance characterization plus a design horse race). The draft is close to submission-ready; the issues below are framing tightness and presentation, not structural defects.

## Major issues (P1)

### M1 — Hardcoded section numbers (Section~5 actually points to subsection 5.2)
- **Category:** Citation Format / Cross-references
- **Location:** `main.tex:41`; `sections/01_introduction.tex:53,83`; `sections/03_scoring_rules.tex:7,43`; `sections/06_design_comparison.tex:10,23`; `sections/08_appendix.tex:290,301`
- **Quote:** `"two further count-loss rules---Hamming and Chebyshev distance---violate it and Section~5 takes them up as the boundary of the approach."`
- **Problem:** Six instances say "Section~5" for the Hamming/Chebyshev treatment, but those rules in fact live in subsection 5.2 of the Discussion. The literal reference resolves (Section 5 *contains* 5.2), but it reads as if there is a standalone Section 5 dedicated to those rules. Similar hardcoding affects Section~2 (`03_scoring_rules.tex:132`), Section~3 (`06_design_comparison.tex:23`), and Section~4 (`08_appendix.tex:301`). Section reordering would silently break all of these.
- **Fix:** Add `\label{sec:hamming-chebyshev}` to subsection 5.2 (line 76 of `07_discussion.tex`) and similar labels to every section/subsection. Replace each literal "Section~5" with `Section~\ref{sec:hamming-chebyshev}` (or `\Cref{...}` if using cleveref). Apply the same treatment to all remaining hardcoded section numbers.

### M2 — Unimodality of the Manhattan threshold optimum is asserted but not proved
- **Category:** Internal Consistency / math rigor
- **Location:** `sections/03_scoring_rules.tex:275`; appendix proof at `08_appendix.tex:245-282`
- **Quote:** `"Each sharp coordinate bound is then the optimum, over c in [0,1], of a function that is unimodal in c, so it is the solution of a single monotone scalar equation."`
- **Problem:** Section 3.4 leans on unimodality to justify the one-dimensional root-find used to compute Manhattan bounds (and the entire design exercise depends on this computation). The appendix proof of Proposition 4 derives the threshold representation but stops short of proving the optimization is unimodal in *c*. A referee asking "why one-dimensional?" will not find the answer in the paper.
- **Fix:** Add a short lemma or remark in `app:manhattan-proof` proving unimodality of the per-coordinate sup/inf as a function of *c*. The argument is short: the inverse binomial CDF is monotone in *p* at each fixed *c*, and the simplex normalization makes the relevant slice unimodal. If a clean proof is too long, cite the property explicitly and point to the verification script.

### M3 — Abstract collapses analytic crossover (in *m*) with empirical crossover (in *α*)
- **Category:** Internal Consistency / framing
- **Location:** `main.tex:42`
- **Quote:** `"squared-distance scoring when beliefs are concentrated on a few categories, frequency-guessing scoring when they are balanced across them, with a crossover between the regimes that is established analytically for the closed-form rules and quantified by a design comparison."`
- **Problem:** Corollary 1 establishes a crossover *analytically* in *m* = number of categories with positive reported counts. The empirical step "*m* tracks belief concentration *α*" is the simulation result of §4. The abstract reads as if the crossover in *α* itself is analytical, which is stronger than the corollary delivers. The introduction (`01_introduction.tex:50`) is more careful — bring the abstract in line.
- **Fix:** Rephrase abstract sentence to: "...with a crossover that is established analytically in report concentration and quantified, in belief concentration, by a design comparison." Or: "...with a crossover between the regimes; the analytical comparison shows it occurs in report concentration, and the design comparison shows that report concentration tracks belief concentration." This stays one sentence and matches the body.

### M4 — Systematic UK/US spelling inconsistency
- **Category:** Academic Tone / style
- **Location:** `sections/01_introduction.tex:47` (centerpiece); `sections/03_scoring_rules.tex:12,24,243` (minimize); `sections/07_discussion.tex:26` (minimizes); `sections/08_appendix.tex:23` (minimizes), `:299` (behavior); `sections/06_design_comparison.tex:29,55,56` (favourable, favours), `:93` (minimises)
- **Quote (sample):** `"...minimises worst-case regret..."` (UK, §4); `"...minimize a separable objective..."` (US, §3); `"...behavioural premise..."` (UK, §5); `"...not simulations of subject behavior..."` (US, appendix); `"...the centerpiece result..."` (US, intro)
- **Problem:** Mixed conventions across files. Body uses British "behaviour", "favourable", "favours", "centre-biased", "minimises". Other places use American "centerpiece", "minimize", "behavior", "optimizing", "characterize". Five-plus instances escalate this from minor to one Major.
- **Fix:** Pick one variant. Paper is mostly UK (target journals like *Economic Journal*, *European Economic Review*, *Experimental Economics* are UK-style). Globally replace: `centerpiece` → `centrepiece`, `minimize(s)/minimizing` → `minimise(s)/minimising`, `optimize(s)/optimizing` → `optimise(s)/optimising`, `characterize(s)/characterization(s)` → `characterise(s)/characterisation(s)`, `behavior` → `behaviour`. Note: do not change `characterization` inside cited paper titles in `references.bib`.

### M5 — Abstract phrase "per-coordinate expected loss" misdescribes frequency-guessing
- **Category:** Internal Consistency / framing
- **Location:** `main.tex:41`
- **Quote:** `"All three are instances of a single structural condition on the per-coordinate expected loss; two further count-loss rules---Hamming and Chebyshev distance---violate it..."`
- **Problem:** For frequency-guessing the objective is the multinomial probability — a *product*, not a per-coordinate expected loss. The body (`03_scoring_rules.tex:17-18`) is explicit: the loss is not additively separable but becomes so after a log transformation. The abstract collapses this and overpromises.
- **Fix:** Rephrase: "All three reduce to a separable resource-allocation problem with a discrete-convex per-coordinate cost (after a log transformation in the frequency-guessing case); two further count-loss rules — Hamming and Chebyshev distance — violate the conditions, and Section 5 takes them up as the boundary of the approach." One sentence, matches the body's exact framing.
